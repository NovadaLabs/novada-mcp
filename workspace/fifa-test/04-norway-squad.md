The **Norway men's national football team** ([Norwegian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norwegian_language): *Norges herrelandslag i fotball*, or informally *Landslaget*) represents [Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway) in men's international [football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_football), and is controlled by the [Norwegian Football Federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norwegian_Football_Federation), the governing body for [football in Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Football_in_Norway). Norway's home ground is [Ullevaal Stadion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ullevaal_Stadion) in [Oslo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oslo), and their head coach is [Ståle Solbakken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St%C3%A5le_Solbakken). Norway has participated in the [FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup) four times ([1938](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1938_FIFA_World_Cup), [1994](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup), [1998](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup) and [2026](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_FIFA_World_Cup)), and once in the [UEFA European Championship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_European_Championship) ([2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2000)).
Norway is one of only three national teams with a winning record against [Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team), and the only team to never have lost against them, with two wins and two draws in four matches, including a [1998 World Cup group stage match](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup_Group_A).[4]
After Euro 2000, Norway missed the next twelve major tournaments, until they qualified for the [2026 FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_FIFA_World_Cup).[5][6][7]

## History

Norway's performances in international football have usually been weaker than those of their [Scandinavian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavia) neighbours [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden_men%27s_national_football_team) and [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark_national_football_team), but they did have a golden age in the late 1930s. An Olympic team achieved third place in the [1936 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Football_at_the_1936_Summer_Olympics), after beating hosts [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany_national_football_team) earlier in the tournament. Norway also qualified for the [1938 FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1938_FIFA_World_Cup), where they lost 2–1 after extra time against eventual champions [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy_national_football_team), with this being their last appearance until [1994](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup).
In the post-war years, up to and including the 1980s, Norway was usually considered one of the weaker teams in Europe.[*[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)*] They never qualified for a World Cup or European Championship during this period, usually finishing near the bottom of their qualifying group. Despite this, Norway achieved victories such as a 3–0 win against [Yugoslavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yugoslavia_national_football_team) in 1965, a 1–0 away win against [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France_national_football_team) in 1968, and a 2–1 victory against [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England_national_football_team) in 1981 that prompted radio commentator [Bjørge Lillelien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rge_Lillelien)'s famous "Your boys took a hell of a beating" rant.[8]
Norway had their most successful period from 1990 to 1998 under the legendary coach [Egil "Drillo" Olsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egil_Olsen), with the team once being [ranked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FIFA_Men%27s_World_Rankings) No. 2. Olsen started his training career with Norway with a 6–1 home victory against [Cameroon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon_national_football_team) on 31 October 1990. In [qualifying for the 1994 World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup_qualification), Norway topped their group, finishing above the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands_national_football_team) and England, beating both teams in the process. During the [finals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup) in the United States, Norway was knocked out in the group stage after a win against [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico_national_football_team), a defeat against Italy and a draw against the [Republic of Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_national_football_team). Norway failed to qualify for the round of 16 as all four teams in the group finished with four points and identical goal difference.
At the [1998 World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998_FIFA_World_Cup) in France, Norway was once again eliminated by Italy in the first round of the knockout stage after finishing second in their group, having drawn against [Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco_national_football_team) and [Scotland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotland_national_football_team) and winning 2–1 against [Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team).
Former under-21 coach [Nils Johan Semb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nils_Johan_Semb) replaced Olsen after the latter's retirement. Under Semb's guidance, Norway qualified for [Euro 2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2000), their last major tournament appearance to date. Semb resigned at the end of an unsuccessful qualifying campaign in 2003 and was replaced by [Åge Hareide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85ge_Hareide). Under Hareide, Norway came close to reaching both the [2006 World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup) and [Euro 2008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2008), but ultimately fell short on both occasions. Norway then failed to win a single game in the entirety of 2008, with Hareide resigning at the end of the year. His replacement, initially on a temporary basis, was Olsen, who began his second spell in charge with an away win against Germany, and subsequently signed a three-year contract. Olsen resigned in September 2013[9] after Norway lost at home to [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland_national_football_team) and had limited chances to qualify for the [2014 World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_FIFA_World_Cup) with one game to spare. He was replaced with [Per-Mathias Høgmo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Per-Mathias_H%C3%B8gmo). Olsen later claimed he was sacked.[10]

#### 2020 - present

During the qualification campaign for [UEFA Euro 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2020), Norway finished third in their [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2020_qualifying_Group_F) behind Spain and Sweden, missing direct qualification for the tournament. The team subsequently contested a playoff semifinal against Serbia in October 2020, but was defeated 1–2 and eliminated from contention. Norway then participated in the [2020–21 UEFA Nations League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020%E2%80%9321_UEFA_Nations_League) alongside Austria, Romania, and Northern Ireland, securing second place in the group and thereby retaining its position in [League B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020%E2%80%9321_UEFA_Nations_League_B) for the following edition.
On December 3, 2020, former national team player [Ståle Solbakken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St%C3%A5le_Solbakken) was appointed head coach following the departure of [Lars Lagerbäck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lars_Lagerb%C3%A4ck). In March 2021, Solbakken named [Martin Ødegaard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_%C3%98degaard) as team captain. Norway failed to qualify for both the [2022 FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_FIFA_World_Cup) and [UEFA Euro 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2024). In the [World Cup qualifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_FIFA_World_Cup_qualification_%E2%80%93_UEFA_Group_G), they finished third in a group with the Netherlands and Turkey. Later, in the [Euro 2024 qualifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2024_qualifying_Group_A), they ended behind Spain and Scotland, missing out again. At the [2024–25 UEFA Nations League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024%E2%80%9325_UEFA_Nations_League), Norway clinched direct promotion to [League A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026%E2%80%9327_UEFA_Nations_League_A) after topping their group.
On November 16, 2025, Norway secured qualification for the [2026 FIFA World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_FIFA_World_Cup), ending a 28-year absence from the tournament. The team won all eight of its qualifying matches and finished first in its [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_FIFA_World_Cup_qualification_%E2%80%93_UEFA_Group_I). The campaign concluded with a 4–1 victory over [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy_national_football_team) in Milan, marking Italy’s first-ever home defeat in a World Cup qualifying group-stage match. [Erling Haaland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erling_Haaland) emerged as the [leading scorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_FIFA_World_Cup_qualification#Top_goalscorers) across all qualifiers with 16 goals. For the tournament, Norway was drawn into [Group I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_FIFA_World_Cup_Group_I) alongside Iraq, Senegal, and France.
On June 17, 2026, Norway opened its World Cup campaign with a 4–1 victory over [Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_national_football_team), highlighted by two goals from Haaland, who became the first Norwegian player to score multiple goals in a World Cup match. It was also the first time Norway had scored more than two goals in a World Cup fixture. The team followed up with a 3–2 win against [Senegal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senegal_national_football_team), securing advancement to the [Round of 32](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_FIFA_World_Cup#Round_of_32) prior to its final group-stage match against [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France_national_football_team).

## Team image

### Crest

Norway has used the [national flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Norway) on a white circle as their badge since the 1920s. In May 2008 the [NFF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norwegian_Football_Federation) unveiled a new crest, a [Viking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikings)-style Dragon wrapped around the NFF logo. After massive public pressure the crest was dropped.[11] In the 1980s and the 1990s, Norway used the NFF logo in the opposite breast of the shirt together with the national flag on a white circle. On 12 December 2014, a new crest was presented. The crest primarily features the national flag; in addition, there are two lions taken from the [Norwegian coat of arms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Norway) on the top. The lions face each other while holding a blue miniature of the NFF logo, and between the lions and above the NFF logo is the word "NORGE" (Norway) in blue letters.[12] They also wore a special gold version of their logo against Italy on 16 November 2025, to commemorate their qualification for World Cup 2026 after a 28-year wait.

### Kit suppliers

[Adidas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adidas) supplied Norway's kit from 1992 to 1996, with [Umbro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umbro) taking over until 2014. On 10 September 2014, the NFF and [Nike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nike,_Inc.) announced a new partnership, that made the sportswear provider the official Norwegian team kit supplier from 1 January 2015.[13]
